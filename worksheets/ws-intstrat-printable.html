    Worksheet Strategies and Practice for Integration
    

    Objectives

    • Choose appropriate strategies for integration.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Evaluate indefinite and definite integrals.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Summary of integration techniques.

    1. Simplify the integrand if possible, and use procedural rules to break down the integral (distribute across sums, pull out constants, etc.)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Use the basic formulas like Formulas 1–20 in your table of integrals on reference page 6 (back pages). These are worth memorizing.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Make any substitution that will transform the integral into a simpler one.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Classify the integrand by “form”:
      🔗
      1. Integration by parts.
        🔗
        1. Polynomials are a good choice of \(u\) because they get simpler when differentiating.
          🔗
          🔗
        2. Inverse functions like \(\ln\text{,}\) \(\arcsin\text{,}\) \(\arctan\) are also a good choice of \(u\) because they get simpler when differentiating.
          🔗
          🔗
        3. Remember also that the product of an exponential and a sine or cosine can be integrated by parts twice.
          🔗
          🔗
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Trigonometric forms:
        🔗
        1. \(\int \sin^m x \cos^n x \,dx\text{:}\)
          🔗
          • If \(m\) is odd, save \(\sin x\,dx\) and let \(u=\cos x\) .
            🔗
            🔗
          • If \(n\) is odd, save \(\cos x\,dx\) and let \(u=\sin x\) .
            🔗
            🔗
          • If \(m\) and \(n\) are even, use half-angle identities to write in terms of \(\cos 2x\) and repeat.
            🔗
            🔗
          🔗
          🔗
        2. \(\int \tan^m x \sec^n x \,dx\text{:}\)
          🔗
          • If \(m\) is odd, save \(\sec x\tan x\,dx\) and let \(u=\sec x\text{.}\)
            🔗
            🔗
          • If \(n\) is even, save \(\sec^2 x\,dx\) and let \(u=\tan x\text{.}\)
            🔗
            🔗
          • If \(m\) is even and \(n\) is odd, use the identity \(1 + \tan^2 x = \sec^2x\) to write the integrand in terms of odd powers of secant. Then use the reduction formula (Formula 77 on reference page 9).
            🔗
            🔗
          🔗
          🔗
        🔗
        🔗
      3. Radical forms: try a trigonometric substitution.
        🔗
        1. Form \(\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}\text{:}\) let \(x=a \sin \theta\text{,}\) so \(dx = a \cos\theta\,d\theta\) and \(\sqrt{a^2-x^2} = a \cos \theta\text{.}\)
          🔗
          🔗
        2. Form \(\sqrt{a^2 + x^2}\text{:}\) let \(x=a \tan \theta\text{,}\) so \(dx = a \sec^2\theta\,d\theta\) and \(\sqrt{a^2+x^2} = a \sec \theta\text{.}\)
          🔗
          🔗
        3. Form \(\sqrt{x^2-a^2}\text{:}\) let \(x=a \sec \theta\text{,}\) so \(dx = a \sec\theta\tan\theta\,d\theta\) and \(\sqrt{x^2-a^2} = a \tan \theta\) .
          🔗
          🔗
        🔗
        🔗
      4. Rational forms: try partial fraction decomposition after factoring the denominator.
        🔗
        1. For each power of linear factor \((x-a)^r\) of the denominator, the partial fraction decomposition contains terms of the form
          \begin{equation*} \frac{A_1}{x-a} + \frac{A_2}{(x-a)^2} + \dots + \frac{A_r}{(x-a)^r} \end{equation*}
          (This includes the case \(r=1\) where there is just a single factor of \(x-a\) in the denominator of the original function and a single summand \(A/(x-a)\) .)
          🔗
          🔗
        2. For each power of an irreducible quadratic factor of the form \((x^2 + bx+c)^r\text{,}\) the partial fraction decomposition contains terms of the form
          \begin{equation*} \frac{B_1x+C_1}{x^2 + bx+c} + \frac{B_2x + C_2}{(x^2 + bx+c)^2} + \dots + \frac{B_rx + C_r}{(x^2 + bx+c)^r} \end{equation*}
          (Again, if \(r=1\) there is only one summand.)
          🔗
          🔗
        3. As a check, remember the number of unknowns must be equal to the degree of the denominator to guarantee a unique solution.
          🔗
          🔗
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Still stuck? Try some of these.
      🔗
      1. Manipulate the integrand:
        🔗
        • Multiply by \(1\) with some clever choice of numerator and denominator
          🔗
          🔗
        • Rationalize the numerator.
          🔗
          🔗
        • Rationalize the denominator.
          🔗
          🔗
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Relate the problem to a previously worked one.
        🔗
        🔗
      3. Pick something and substitute that.
        🔗
        🔗
      4. Consult a table of integrals or computer software.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    1.

    \(\displaystyle\int\frac{2x-1}{(x-x^2)}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Use a partial fraction decomposition
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x-1}{x-x^2} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{B}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    \(\displaystyle\int x \sec 2x^2 \,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(u=2x^2\) and \(du = 4x\,dx\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\frac{1}{4}\int \sec u\,du\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    \(\displaystyle\int e^x \cot e^x \,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(u=e^x\) and \(du = e^x\,dx\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\int \cot u\,du\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{\tan(\ln x)}{x}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(u = \ln x\) and \(du = \frac{1}{x}\,dx\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\int \tan u \,du\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{3 + 2 \sin t}{\cos t} \,dt\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Break it up:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \int \frac{3 + 2 \sin t}{\cos t} \,dt &= \int\left(\frac{3}{\Cos{t}} + \frac{2 \Sin{t}}{\Cos{t}}\right)\,dt \\ &= 3\int \sec{t}\,dt + 2 \int \tan{t}\,dt \end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1+e^x}{1-e^x}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(u=e^x\text{,}\) so \(x=\ln u\) and \(dx = \frac{1}{u}\,du\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\int \frac{1+u}{1-u}\cdot\frac{1}{u} \,du\text{.}\) Now you can do partial fractions on the integrand \(\frac{1+u}{u-u^2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{2t^2\,dt}{1-t^6}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    The denominator factors as
    \begin{equation*} 1-t^6 = (1-t^3)(1+t^3) = (1-t)(1+t+t^2)(1+t)(1-t+t^2) \end{equation*}
    and the two quadratic factors are irreducible (use the discriminant to show that they have non-real roots). So the partial fraction decomposition takes the form
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1-t^6} = \frac{A}{1-t} + \frac{B}{1+t} + \frac{Cx + D}{1+t+t^2} + \frac{Ex+F}{1-t+t^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    8.

    \(\displaystyle\int\frac{dx}{x^2 + 2x + 2}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Complete the square in the denominator: \(x^2 + 2x + 2 = (x+1)^2 + 1\text{.}\) Then substitute \(u=x+1\text{,}\) \(du=dx\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\int\frac{du}{u^2+1}\) .
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    9.

    \(\displaystyle\int \sin^5 x \cos^2 x\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(u=\Cos{x}\) and \(du = -\Sin{x}\,dx\text{.}\) The integral becomes
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^5 x \cos^2 x\,dx = \int \sin^4(x)\cos^2(x) \cdot\sin(x)\,dx = \int (1-u^2)^2u^2\,du \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    10.

    \(\displaystyle\int \sin^4 x \cos^2 x\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Use the half-angle identities.
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^4 x \cos^2 x\,dx = \int \left(\frac{1-\cos 2x}{2}\right)^2\left(\frac{1+\cos{2x}}{2}\right)\,dx \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    11.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{\sqrt{1-x^2}}{x}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(u = \sqrt{1-x^2}\) The integral becomes \(\int \frac{u^2\,du}{1-u^2}\text{.}\) Now do partial fractions.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    12.

    \(\displaystyle\int x \sec^{-1} x \,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Use integration by parts with \(u = \sec^{-1}x\text{.}\) Then \(du=\frac{1}{x \sqrt{x^2-1}}\) , \(dv = x\,dx\text{,}\) and \(v = \frac{1}{2} x^2\text{.}\) So the integral becomes
    \begin{equation*} \int x \sec^{-1} x \,dx = \frac{1}{2}x^2\sec^{-1}x - \int \frac{x}{2\sqrt{x^2-1}}\,dx \end{equation*}
    Now substitute \(u = x^2-1\text{,}\) \(du=2x\,dx\) in the remaining integral.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    13.

    \(\displaystyle\int \tan^4 x\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Use the identity \(t + \tan^2(x) = \sec^2(x)\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \int \tan^4 x\,dx &= \int \tan^2(x)\left(\sec^2(x) -1 \right) \,dx \\ &= \int \left(\tan^2(x)\sec^2(x) -\tan^2(x) \right) \,dx \\ &= \int \left(\tan^2(x)\sec^2(x) -\left(\sec^2(x)-1\right)\right) \,dx \\ &= \int \left(\tan^2(x)-1\right)\sec^2(x)\,dx + \int1 \,dx \\ \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Now substitute \(u=\tan(x)\text{,}\) \(du=\sec^2(x)\,dx\) in the first integral.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    14.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{\arctan x}{1 + x^2}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(u= \arctan x\) and \(du= \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\int u \,du\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    15.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{\arctan x}{(1 + x^2)^3}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(y=\arctan x\) and \(dy=\frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx\text{.}\) Then \(x^2 + 1 = \tan^2 y +1 = \sec^2y\) so the integral becomes
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{y}{\sec^4 y}\,dy = \int y \cos^4 y \,du \end{equation*}
    Now use the half-angle identities to write \(\cos^4y\) as a combination of \(\Cos{2y}\) and \(\Cos{4y}\text{.}\) Then integrate by parts with \(u=y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    16.

    \(\displaystyle\int \ln\sqrt{1+x^2}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    First, the square root in the logarithm is a red herring:
    \begin{equation*} \ln{\sqrt{1+x^2}} = \frac{1}{2}{\ln{\left(1+x^2\right)}} \end{equation*}
    Do integration by parts with \(u =\frac{1}{2}\ln{\left(1+x^2\right)}\text{,}\) \(du = \frac{x}{1+x^2}\,dx\text{,}\) \(dv = dx\) , \(v=x\text{.}\) The integral becomes
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2}x\ln{\left(1+x^2\right)} - \int \frac{x^2}{1+x^2}\,dx \end{equation*}
    Now rewrite \(\frac{x^2}{1+x^2} = 1 - \frac{1}{1+x^2}\) (long division).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    17.

    \(\displaystyle\int x\ln\sqrt{1+x^2}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Again the logarithmic identities reduce the integral to \(\frac{1}{2}\int x\ln\left(1+x^2\right)\,dx\text{.}\) Substitute \(u=1+x^2\) and \(du = 2x\,dx\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\frac{1}{4}\int \ln{u} \,du\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    18.

    \(\displaystyle\int \arcsin\sqrt{x}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    This is one of those “stab in the dark” integrals were you pick something complicated and substitute for it. Substitute \(y=\sqrt{x}\text{,}\) so \(x = y^2\) and \(dx = 2y\,dy\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\int 2 y \arcsin y\,dy\text{.}\) Now integrate by parts with \(u=\arcsin y\) and \(dv = 2y\text{.}\) So \(du = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-y^2}}\,dy\) and \(v = y^2\text{.}\) The integral becomes
    \begin{equation*} y^2 \arcsin y - \int\frac{y^2}{\sqrt{1-y^2}}\,dy \end{equation*}
    Now do a trig substitution \(y=\sin \theta\) so \(\sqrt{1-y^2} = \cos\theta\) and \(dy = \cos\theta\,d\theta\text{.}\) The remaining integral becomes \(\int\sin^2\theta\,d\theta\text{.}\) This can be done with a half-angle identity.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    19.

    \(\displaystyle\int \sqrt{\tan x}\,dx\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Substitute \(y=\sqrt{\tan x}\text{,}\) so \(x = \ArcTan{y^2}\text{,}\) and \(dx = \frac{2y}{1 + y^4}\,dy\text{.}\) The integral becomes \(\int\frac{2y^2}{1+y^4}\,dy\text{.}\) Now do partial fractions; the denominator factors as
    \begin{equation*} y^4 + 1 = -\left(-y^2+\sqrt{2} y-1\right) \left(y^2+\sqrt{2} y+1\right) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    20.

    \(\displaystyle\int \frac{dx}{x^6 + 1}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Partial fractions. The denominator factors as
    \begin{equation*} x^6+1 = (x^2+1)(x^4-x^2 + 1) = (x^2+1)(x^2 - \sqrt{3}x + 1)(x^2 +\sqrt{3}x + 1) \end{equation*}
